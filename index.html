Lakota Land Loss, 1851–Present

The Očhéthi Šakówiŋ

Before 1851

The territory of the Očhéthi Šakówiŋ — the Seven Council Fires — extended across the Northern Great Plains from the Missouri River west into the Rocky Mountain foothills, and from the Platte River north into present-day Canada. At its heart stood the Black Hills: Pahá Sápa, the center of the Lakota world.

The confederacy comprised seven distinct nations: the four Dakota (Mdewakanton, Wahpekute, Wahpeton, Sisíthuŋwaŋ), the two Nakota (Yankton, Yanktonai), and the Teton Lakota — whose bands, including the Hunkpapa and Sicangu, held the westernmost territory. Their relationship to this land was collective, relational, and non-commodified. No document bounded it. That was about to change.

Fort Laramie, 1851

Horse Creek Treaty — September 17, 1851

The 1851 Fort Laramie Treaty recognized a Lakota homeland encompassing more than 60 million acres centered on the Black Hills, spanning present-day North Dakota, South Dakota, Montana, Wyoming, and Nebraska. The promise was recorded in the De Smet map, drawn by Jesuit missionary Pierre-Jean De Smet at the council's conclusion — the surviving cartographic record of that promise.

The treaty was not fully ratified as agreed. The Senate unilaterally reduced the promised annuity period from fifty to ten years — a change not returned to the original signatories for their consent. This map also shows the five neighboring nations whose boundaries were defined simultaneously: a US administrative act, bounding Indigenous territories on its own terms.

Great Sioux Reservation, 1868

Fort Laramie Treaty — April 29, 1868

Following Red Cloud's War — in which Lakota forces successfully closed the Bozeman Trail — the 1868 treaty established the Great Sioux Reservation: all land west of the Missouri River in present-day South Dakota, including the Black Hills. Article XII required three-quarters of all adult male Lakota to consent to any future cession.

"No treaty for the cession of any portion of the reservation shall be of any validity unless signed by at least three-fourths of all adult male Indians."

Six years later, gold was discovered. In 1874, Custer led 1,200 men into the Black Hills in direct violation of the treaty. By 1875, there were an estimated 1,500 illegal miners in the Hills. The government attempted to purchase them rather than enforce its own obligations.

The Taking, 1877–1889

Act of February 28, 1877 · Sioux Act 1889 · Dawes Act 1887

When Lakota leaders refused to sell, Congress cut off all food rations guaranteed under the 1868 treaty. The Manypenny Commission gathered 230 signatures — 10% of adult male Lakota. Three-quarters were required. On February 28, 1877, the United States officially seized 7 million acres of the Black Hills.

The Sioux Act of 1889 broke the single reservation into six units and opened 9 million acres to white settlement. The Dawes Act imposed individual 160-acre allotments; remaining land was declared "surplus" and sold. Between 1887 and 1934, Native American land holdings nationally collapsed from 138 to 48 million acres.

What Remains

Current tribal lands against the 1851 boundary

In 1980, the Supreme Court ruled unanimously that the Black Hills seizure was an unconstitutional taking. The settlement — now more than $1.8 billion — has been refused for over four decades.

"The Black Hills are not for sale. We will never sell out our holy lands." — Frank Star Comes Out, Oglala Sioux Tribal President

The Hills remain in federal and state hands. What the map shows as current tribal lands is what survives of a territory once spanning 60 million acres: a checkerboard of trust and fee land, the reservation boundary intact but its territorial coherence perforated by 150 years of deliberate legal instruments.

Conclusion

A stratigraphic document

These maps are a sequence of legal instruments rendered as geography. The land did not change. The documents changed the land.

The central question is not only how much was taken, but through what mechanisms. This makes legible the tools of encroachment, not just its effects.
